Biography

NChandra Shekhar Azad, born as Chandra Shekhar Tiwari on July 23, 1906, was an Indian revolutionary and a prominent figure in the Indian independence movement against British colonial rule. His indomitable spirit, courage, and dedication to the cause of freedom made him a legendary figure in Indian history. Azad was born in Bhavra village, in present-day Alirajpur district of Madhya Pradesh, India. From an early age, he was deeply influenced by the prevailing nationalist sentiments and the fervor for independence. He was particularly inspired by the sacrifices of revolutionaries like Bhagat Singh, Ram Prasad Bismil, and Ashfaqulla Khan. In his youth, Azad joined the Non-Cooperation Movement led by Mahatma Gandhi in 1920, but later he became disillusioned with the Gandhian ideology, feeling that more radical action was necessary to achieve independence. He joined the Hindustan Republican Association (HRA), which later became the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA), a revolutionary organization dedicated to achieving independence through armed struggle. Azad's role in the HSRA was crucial, and he became one of its most prominent leaders. He participated in several acts of revolutionary violence against British officials and institutions. He was involved in the Kakori Conspiracy of 1925, in which members of the HSRA looted a train carrying government funds near Kakori, Uttar Pradesh, to finance their revolutionary activities. The incident brought Azad to the forefront of the independence movement. One of the most defining aspects of Azad's persona was his commitment to never being captured alive by the British authorities. He took on the name "Azad," which means "free" in Urdu, to symbolize his resolve to live and die as a free man. He managed to evade capture for several years, constantly moving from one place to another, organizing and planning revolutionary activities. However, Azad's life was cut short at a young age. On February 27, 1931, the police surrounded him in Alfred Park (now Azad Park) in Allahabad. Despite being heavily outnumbered, Azad refused to surrender and fought bravely. Realizing that he was about to be captured, he shot himself with his last bullet, keeping his pledge of never being caught alive. He was only 24 years old at the time of his death. Chandra Shekhar Azad's martyrdom immortalized him as a symbol of courage, defiance, and sacrifice in the struggle for Indian independence. His legacy continues to inspire generations of Indians to fight against oppression and injustice. Numerous memorials, parks, and institutions across India bear his name, ensuring that his memory remains eternally engraved in the annals of Indian history.
